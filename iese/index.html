Topic 1 of 8
Durable Consumer Goods

Small Home Appliances: Braun

Braun operates in the durable consumer goods sector, where product cycles are long, replacement rates are low, and the challenge of sustaining growth is constant. Unlike fast-moving consumer goods, every sale must justify a premium in a market where basic functionality has been commoditised.

This case explores the real-world limitations of market research in durable goods: what happens when the data tells you one thing but the market behaves differently? We will examine how to react when budgets are drastically cut and resources become scarce, forcing leaders to make difficult trade-offs between innovation, marketing, and distribution.

Most critically, we will analyse how to respond when a new competitor emerges and directly threatens your market leadership position. What are the strategic options? How do you defend share without destroying margins? When should you fight and when should you adapt?

Key Questions

  • What are the real limitations of market research, and when should you trust instinct over data?
  • How do you react strategically when your budget is drastically reduced?
  • What is the right response when a competitor threatens your market leadership?
Topic 2 of 8
Launch / Market Introduction

Launch of Chupa Chups in the USA

Chupa Chups had conquered markets across Europe and Asia, but the United States proved to be a different beast entirely. Multiple previous attempts to enter the US market had failed. This case examines how to crack the world's most competitive consumer market when you have no budget and limited knowledge of local dynamics.

At the heart of the story is the culture of a family-owned company attempting to compete against established American giants. We will explore how to successfully introduce a mass consumer product in the USA without significant financial resources, the critical decisions around range expansion to build critical mass, and the commercial organisation choices that made or broke the strategy.

The case also examines the distribution channel options available and the growth alternatives considered: M&A versus alliances and joint ventures. Each path carried different risks, resource requirements, and cultural implications for a family business accustomed to doing things its own way.

Key Questions

  • How do you enter the US market with no budget and after multiple failed attempts?
  • What role does family business culture play in international expansion decisions?
  • When is M&A the right growth path versus alliances or joint ventures?
  • How do you build critical mass through range expansion in a new market?
Topic 3 of 8
Commodity Strategy

Commodity Strategy: Marcilla Coffee

Coffee is the ultimate commodity: priced by the kilo on international markets, with inherently low margins. Marcilla operated within the culture of its Dutch parent company, Douwe Egberts, which was built on the discipline of managing scarce resources in a low-profitability-per-kilo environment.

The competitive landscape was dominated by multinational giants: Nestlé and General Foods held commanding positions. This case examines how a smaller player can compete and survive against dominant multinationals in a commodity market, with particular focus on the development of the instant coffee segment and the challenge of competing against Nescafé, which held approximately 95% market share.

We will also explore the Nespresso disruption, a patent-protected system that transformed coffee consumption over 30 years (from the 1970s/80s onwards), and the practical experiences and lessons learned from working with major food retailers and the dynamics of large-scale food distribution.

Key Questions

  • How do you compete against a brand with 95% market share (Nescafé)?
  • What does managing a commodity business with a Dutch "scarce resources" culture teach us?
  • How did Nespresso's 30-year patent disrupt an entire industry?
  • What are the key lessons from navigating large-scale food retail distribution?
Topic 4 of 8
FMCG / Fast-Moving Consumer Goods

PepsiCo Matutano (Frito-Lay)

The savoury snacks market was experiencing a dramatic slowdown: growth rates dropped from +30% and +20% in the 1970s-80s to just +9% and +6% in the 1990s-2000s. This raised serious doubts about research and market intelligence. How do you make an accurate diagnosis? What factors drive a market deceleration: shifting trends, changing consumption habits, the growing tension between snacking culture and health-conscious eating?

This case highlights the critical importance of being market-savvy. The power of commercial intuition, that fine-tuned business instinct exemplified by breakthrough moves like the TAZOS promotion (featuring Dragon Ball), which became a cultural phenomenon. We will explore the engineering of distribution route design as a competitive weapon and the practical experiences of working with large-scale food retailers.

The case also addresses the human dimension of leadership: navigating antagonism with a superior, the politics of multinational company representation, promotional strategy and in-store execution, diversity challenges, and the tension between authoritarian and participative management styles.

Key Questions

  • How do you diagnose the real causes behind a market growth slowdown?
  • What is the role of commercial intuition versus formal market research?
  • How do you design distribution routes as a competitive advantage?
  • How do you navigate leadership tensions: authoritarian versus participative styles?
Topic 5 of 8
Former State Monopoly

Tabacalera / Altadis

Tabacalera was a former state monopoly with all the cultural baggage that implies: a civil servant mentality, a workforce shaped by decades without competition, and significant ethical challenges. The company faced a fundamental crisis in its value proposition that demanded an honest diagnosis before any transformation could begin.

The turnaround required addressing a severe deficit in human capital: high turnover, low retention, and the challenge of attracting talent to a company with a "loser" culture. We will examine how leadership focused the organisation around the launch of three key brands, restructured the commercial organisation and channel strategy, and began the painful process of cultural transformation.

The case also explores how to overcome advertising restrictions in a heavily regulated industry through lifestyle branding, the commercial exploitation of early successes to build momentum, and the broader question of how a former monopoly can reinvent itself when its entire operating model was built on state protection.

Key Questions

  • How do you transform an organisation with a civil servant, "loser" culture?
  • How do you diagnose and address a fundamental crisis in value proposition?
  • How do you overcome advertising restrictions through lifestyle branding?
  • How do you build momentum by exploiting initial successes?
Topic 6 of 8
Former State Monopoly / Turnaround

Telefónica 1: Terra Lycos Turnaround

Telefónica, as a former state monopoly, carried a deeply ingrained engineering-driven culture. This case begins with the clash between that culture and the FMCG-style commercial experience needed for consumer-facing products: from the launch of ADSL (including presenting TV advertising campaigns) to the question of whether the organisation was truly 100% customer-oriented.

The centrepiece of the case is the presidency of Terra Lycos: a turnaround challenge of enormous proportions. We will examine the strategic question of whether to pursue an alliance with Microsoft, the rationalisation of a sprawling portfolio of digital assets, and the eventual sale of Lycos. Each decision was taken under intense pressure from markets, investors, and internal stakeholders.

The case also explores the practical dimensions of managing a complex corporate structure, including fiscal expertise and the challenge of bridging the gap between telecoms engineers and consumer marketers within a single organisation.

Key Questions

  • How do you bridge the cultural gap between engineers and consumer marketers?
  • When is an alliance (e.g. with Microsoft) the right strategic move versus going alone?
  • How do you rationalise a portfolio and execute a turnaround under intense pressure?
  • What does "100% customer orientation" actually mean in a former monopoly?
Topic 7 of 8
Brand Image & Institutional Relations

Telefónica 2: Brand Image Recovery & Institutional Relations in Catalonia

Telefónica Catalonia faced a unique challenge: transforming a brand image from deeply negative to positive, fighting against powerful political and cultural headwinds. The question was fundamental: how do you demonstrate genuine commitment to a territory that views you with suspicion?

The organisational response was comprehensive. A multi-stakeholder advisory council was established. A Catalan-language call centre was created. An R&D centre and corporate university were set up in the region. The fibre optic network rollout (including antenna deployment) was accelerated. Telefónica played a key role in Barcelona's candidacy for the Mobile World Congress. The company embedded itself in every major local institution and established a priority customer service channel.

This case is a masterclass in institutional relations and stakeholder management in a politically charged environment. Every decision, from which language the call centre operated in to which local institutions to engage with, carried strategic weight far beyond its operational impact.

Key Questions

  • How do you turn a negative brand image positive when fighting against political and cultural headwinds?
  • What concrete actions demonstrate genuine corporate commitment to a territory?
  • How do you manage a multi-stakeholder political advisory council?
  • What role did the Mobile World Congress candidacy play in the brand recovery?
Topic 8 of 8
Leadership & Personal Reflection

My Personal Failures: Lessons Learned

In the business world, we talk a great deal about successes but very little about failures. And yet, most experienced executives will admit they have learned far more from their mistakes than from their achievements. This topic breaks with academic convention to offer an honest, personal reflection.

Through real experiences of business failure, wrong decisions, missed opportunities, and errors of judgement, we will explore the lessons that can only be learned when things go wrong. From failed investments to team management mistakes, from strategies that looked brilliant on paper but failed in execution.

This session is unique because it combines strategic theory with personal vulnerability. We will analyse common patterns in business failures, the cognitive biases that lead us to make poor decisions, the importance of resilience, and how to build a culture where mistakes become a source of learning rather than punishment.

Key Questions

  • What patterns repeat in flawed business decisions?
  • How can we create systems that detect errors in time?
  • What is the real value of failure in the development of a leader?
