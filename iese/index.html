Durable Consumer Goods

Small Home Appliances: Braun

Braun, the iconic German brand founded in 1921, revolutionised the small home appliances market with its functionalist design and precision engineering. From electric shavers to food processors, Braun set the quality standard in the premium market segment.

This case examines Braun's strategy in a highly competitive durable goods market: how to maintain premium positioning against pressure from low-cost Asian brands, product lifecycle management, and the decisions around incremental versus disruptive innovation.

We will analyse the integration within the Procter & Gamble group (acquired in 1967 by Gillette, which in turn was acquired by P&G in 2005), the tensions between brand identity and corporate synergies, and how a durable goods company can create sustainable competitive advantages when products last for years.

Key Questions

  • How do you justify a premium price in appliances when basic functionality has been commoditised?
  • What role does design play as a barrier to entry?
  • How do you manage an iconic brand within a large conglomerate without losing its essence?
Topic 2 of 8
Launch & Internationalisation

Launch of Chupa Chups in the USA

Chupa Chups, born in Barcelona in 1958 from the vision of Enric Bernat, achieved extraordinary success across Europe and Asia with a seemingly simple product: a lollipop. With a logo designed by Salvador Dalí and distribution reaching 150 countries, Chupa Chups became a global phenomenon.

However, the North American market presented a unique challenge. This case analyses the US market entry strategy: a market dominated by giants like Hershey's, Mars, and Wrigley, with consolidated distribution channels, different consumer preferences, and its own confectionery culture.

We will study the positioning decisions (children's product versus aspirational), pricing strategy, channel selection, product adaptation versus standardisation, and the lessons learned when a brand that dominates at home tries to conquer the world's most competitive market.

Key Questions

  • Why can a globally successful product fail in a specific market?
  • When should you adapt the product to the local market versus maintain standardisation?
  • How do you compete with local giants that control distribution?

Commodity Strategy

Commodity Strategy: Marcilla Coffee

Coffee is one of the most traded commodities in the world, with prices determined by international markets and a supply chain connecting producers in developing countries with global consumers. In this context, how can a brand create value beyond the raw material price?

Marcilla, a Spanish brand with over a century of history, faced the challenge of competing in a market where the base product is essentially identical. This case examines strategies for escaping the commoditisation trap: creating value through blending, roasting, packaging, branding, and the consumption experience.

We will analyse the competition with private-label brands (which capture a growing share), the arrival of new formats (capsules, specialty coffee), managing the volatility of green coffee prices, and the strategic decisions around vertical integration versus specialisation.

Key Questions

  • How do you differentiate a product when the raw material is a commodity?
  • What is the right strategy in the face of the rise of private-label brands?
  • How do you respond to the capsule disruption (Nespresso) without cannibalising the core business?
Topic 4 of 8
FMCG / Fast-Moving Consumer Goods

Fast-Moving Consumer Goods: Frito-Lay

Frito-Lay, PepsiCo's snacks division, is one of the most successful examples of operational excellence in FMCG. With brands like Lay's, Doritos, Cheetos, and Ruffles, it dominates the savoury snacks market with market shares exceeding 60% in the USA.

This case explores what many consider the world's best Direct Store Delivery (DSD) network: a fleet of thousands of trucks visiting hundreds of thousands of points of sale weekly. This logistics infrastructure is simultaneously its greatest competitive advantage and its largest cost.

We will study the brand portfolio strategy, constant innovation in flavours and formats, distribution management as a competitive weapon, and the dilemma of how to grow when you already dominate the market. We will also address current challenges: the trend towards healthy eating, regulatory pressure on unhealthy snacks, and how a snack giant can reinvent itself.

Key Questions

  • How do you turn distribution into an insurmountable competitive advantage?
  • What is the right strategy when you control 60% of the market?
  • How do you respond to the health and wellness trend without destroying the core business?
Topic 5 of 8
Former State Monopoly

Tabacalera / Altadis

Tabacalera was for decades the Spanish state tobacco monopoly. Its transformation from a bureaucratic public enterprise into Altadis, a listed international group eventually acquired by Imperial Tobacco, is a fascinating case study in privatisation, liberalisation, and corporate transformation.

This case analyses the unique challenges of a former monopoly: how to compete when borders open, how to manage an organisational culture shaped by decades of protection, and how to transform a company accustomed to having no competition into an agile, market-oriented organisation.

We will study the merger with France's Seita to create Altadis, the internationalisation strategy, portfolio decisions (cigarettes, cigars, distribution), managing the growing regulatory challenges against tobacco, and the universal lessons about how former monopolies can (or cannot) survive liberalisation.

Key Questions

  • What advantages and disadvantages does a former monopoly inherit when it is privatised?
  • How do you transform a corporate culture built on state protection?
  • Is an international merger the best response to liberalisation?
Topic 6 of 8
Former State Monopoly / Digital Turnaround

Telefónica 1: Terra Lycos Turnaround

In the late 1990s, Telefónica made an ambitious bet on the internet world with the creation of Terra Networks. The acquisition of Lycos in 2000 for $12.5 billion was one of the largest deals of the dot-com era, just before the bubble burst.

This case examines one of the most complex turnarounds in Spanish business history: how to manage a multi-billion-dollar investment that loses value dramatically, what strategic options remain when the market collapses, and how a large telecommunications group should integrate (or divest) its digital assets.

We will analyse the decisions made under extreme pressure, managing investor and market expectations, the dilemma between persisting with the original strategy or accepting losses and pivoting, and the lessons about diversification, market entry timing, and the dangers of the "new economy".

Key Questions

  • How do you manage an acquisition that loses 90% of its value in two years?
  • When should you persist with a strategy versus accept the losses?
  • What lessons from Terra Lycos remain relevant for today's technology investments?
Topic 7 of 8
Brand Image & Institutional Relations

Telefónica 2: Brand Image Recovery & Institutional Relations in Catalonia

Telefónica, as a former state monopoly, inherited a particular perception in Catalonia: a company associated with Madrid-centric centralisation, perceived as distant and even hostile to the region. In a market where local alternatives capitalised on the sentiment of proximity, Telefónica needed to rebuild its image.

This case analyses a brand recovery strategy in a complex socio-political context: how a large corporation can reconnect with a territory where it is perceived negatively, the institutional relations actions taken, strategic sponsorship, localisation of service and communication, and the delicate balance between adapting to the local context without alienating other markets.

We will study the tactics deployed (from cultural sponsorship to hiring local executives), the effectiveness of each action, and the broader lessons about brand management in contexts where political and identity dimensions weigh as much or more than service quality.

Key Questions

  • How can an "outsider" company earn the trust of a territory with a strong identity of its own?
  • To what extent can institutional relations and sponsorship change brand perception?
  • How do you manage a national brand in a context of territorial tensions?
Topic 8 of 8
Leadership & Personal Reflection

My Personal Failures: Lessons Learned

In the business world, we talk a great deal about successes but very little about failures. And yet, most experienced executives will admit they have learned far more from their mistakes than from their achievements. This topic breaks with academic convention to offer an honest, personal reflection.

Through real experiences of business failure, wrong decisions, missed opportunities, and errors of judgement, we will explore the lessons that can only be learned when things go wrong. From failed investments to team management mistakes, from strategies that looked brilliant on paper but failed in execution.

This session is unique because it combines strategic theory with personal vulnerability. We will analyse common patterns in business failures, the cognitive biases that lead us to make poor decisions, the importance of resilience, and how to build a culture where mistakes become a source of learning rather than punishment.

Key Questions

  • What patterns repeat in flawed business decisions?
  • How can we create systems that detect errors in time?
  • What is the real value of failure in the development of a leader?
